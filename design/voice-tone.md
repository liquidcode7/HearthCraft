# HearthCraft — Voice & Tone Guide

> Authoritative guide for all in-game writing: band member dialogue and banter,
> provisioning requests, the burglar gag, flavour text, inspiration lines, journal
> entries, and any other player-facing text.
>
> Every line of game writing answers to this doc. If in doubt, ask: does this
> sound like something Tolkien could have written? If not, rewrite it.

---

## The Core Rule

**All character text must sound Tolkienesque and must never be anachronistic.**

This is not atmosphere or preference — it is a hard constraint. The game is set
in Middle-earth's Third Age. Every word the player reads should belong there.

---

## Rules of the Register

### 1. No modern idiom or rhythm

Modern slang, colloquial contractions, and contemporary sentence cadence are
all wrong — they pull the player out of the world instantly.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| "Has anyone else *clocked* this?" | *"Have none of you marked it?"* |
| "That's a bit sus." | *"Something here does not sit right."* |
| "Okay, let's go." | *"Right. Let us be off."* |
| "Honestly, same." | *"I know that feeling well."* |

### 2. Plainness over cleverness

Tolkien's humor is dry, grounded, understated — character and attitude, not
wordplay or zingers. The comedy in the burglar gag lands *because* the dwarves
say it earnestly, not because they're winking at the camera.

Avoid: puns, rhetorical flourishes, meta-awareness, modern comic timing.
Prefer: understatement, dry observation, the weight of something said once and
not repeated.

### 3. Race and culture shape voice

Each people has a distinct register. Use it consistently.

| Race / people | Voice notes |
|---|---|
| **Dwarves** | Blunt, proud, gruff, a touch archaic. Direct about what they want. Do not emote easily but mean it when they do. Gravity is their default mode. |
| **Elves** | Graver, more measured. Amused melancholy — they have seen too much of the world's grief to be surprised, but they are not without joy. Long sentences are natural to them; brevity is a choice. |
| **Rangers / Dúnedain** | Spare, plain-spoken. Economy of words. They say what needs saying and no more. Emotions are real but not announced. |
| **Hobbits** | Homely, practical, fond of comfort and good food. Cheerful by default. Talk about meals, weather, and the comforts of home even in bad situations. Their plainness is not stupidity — it is a different set of priorities. |

### 4. Vocabulary discipline

**Favor:** folk, fellow, naught, marked (= noticed), queer (= strange), reckon,
ere (= before), yonder, indeed, well enough, see to it, a good watch, the old
tongue (for elvish/dwarvish references), hearken.

**Avoid:** okay, guys, yeah, cool, right? (rhetorical), totally, honestly,
honestly though, just, literally, basically, you know, no worries.

**Contractions:** period-plain contractions are fine (*it's, that's, we'll,
don't*). Avoid contractions that exist only because modern speech is lazy
(*gonna, wanna, kinda, sorta, dunno*).

Stay readable. Never Renaissance-faire parody. Tolkien himself stayed readable —
you do not need to sound like the King James Bible. The goal is the register of
*The Lord of the Rings*, not *The Silmarillion*.

## The Burglar Gag — a worked example

The hire option always reads: **"Hire a burglar."** Never "hire a hobbit."
The UI is deadpan. The joke lives entirely in band member reactions.

**Wrong (modern):**
> Dagra: *"Wait — is that another one? It's literally always a halfling. Every.
> Single. Time."*

**Right (in register, dwarf voice):**
> Dagra: *"Has anyone else marked that it is always one of the Little Folk?
> I have hired seven burglars in my life and not one was anything else."*

Later progression, same dwarf:
> *"Another halfling. Aye, well. At least this one has sensible boots."*

Much later, affectionate resignation:
> *"I have stopped wondering. Send for the halfling. They always know what they
> are about, even if you cannot see them until they are standing on your foot."*

The comedy deepens *because* they mean it earnestly. The in-world logic is
sound — burgling is a hobbit profession, and they are very good at it. The band
is not making a joke. They are making an observation.

## Tone Fork (open — decide before narrative content is written)

Tolkien holds both registers:
- **Cozy and humane** — warmth against a hard world, the hearth as answer to
  the dark.
- **Quietly tragic** — the Long Defeat; sustaining an effort that may not be
  won; grief carried without complaint.

Which is *dominant* changes how all narrative reads. The burglar gag and
provisioning flavor lean cozy. The elves and the westward-sailing thread lean
tragic. The current design uses both but has not decided which is the spine.
Resolve before the narrative content pass begins.
