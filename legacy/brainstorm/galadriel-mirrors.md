## The Core Idea

A mission type that puts the player *inside* famous canonical events without
the agency-dissonance of WoW-style Timewalking. The Mirror of Galadriel
canonically shows "things that were, things that are, and things that yet may
be" — so a mission that lets a band witness or move through a great event they
cannot alter is not a gameplay contrivance. It's the Mirror doing exactly what
Tolkien said it does. The player is not time-travelling; they are being *shown
a vision*.

**Why this dissolves the usual time-walk problem:** Timewalking feels fake
because you act yet nothing sticks. A *vision* is not supposed to be changeable,
so the lack of consequence stops being a frustrating limitation and becomes the
correct, expected nature of the thing. The player was never an actor in it.
They are a witness.

---

## Mechanical Home

**The outlast / survival fight** (already designed in `design/combat-model.md`).

A Mirror is never won by altering the event — you cannot kill the Balrog; it
is a vision and beyond you anyway. The mission is to *endure the vision* until
it passes: single-axis, unkillable-enemy survival/outlast fight.

Galadriel's Mirrors are the natural home of the outlast-fight type.

## Rewards — Knowledge Only

The one thing you can legitimately carry out of something you witnessed but
couldn't change is *knowing*. Mirrors grant:

- **Rare recipes obtainable no other way.** Fits the top of the rarity ladder:
  singular, non-farmable. A craft remembered from a vision of better days.
  Natural Grandmaster-tier source.
- **Character skills for band members.** Witnessing greatness teaches something.
  Gives the bands a progression source that isn't combat or food, and a reason
  to send *specific members* to a Mirror (deepens who-you-bring decisions).

**Keep Mirrors strictly knowledge-bearing.** No coin, no common loot, or they
blur into ordinary missions. You don't run a Mirror for spoils; you run it to
learn something the world forgot. This also reinforces the melancholy-and-wonder
tone: the only thing salvageable from the irretrievable past is the wisdom of
having seen it.

## Structure and Gating

### Gated to post-Moria / Lothlórien

The Mirror is a basin in Galadriel's garden — unreachable until the bands have
crossed the mountains into Lórien (era 2 of the three-era structure). Mirror
missions cannot exist before the Rivendell hinge. This fixes ordering: cross
Moria as a *present* danger first, then later be *shown* its past.

**The Mirror of the Balrog** becomes a reflection on a crossing you already
survived. Experience, then vision. More poignant than witnessing it cold.

**Fornost & Carn Dûm:** seeded in era 1 as present-day ruins you walk without
understanding. Paid off in era 2 as visions that show how they fell. They
appear twice — places first, then the visions that explain them. Removes the
Angmar/Fornost timeline problem.

### The Mirror as a place, not a menu

A fixed Lothlórien location. Reaching it is an event; returning is a deliberate
campaign expedition — distinct from idle foraging. Keeps Lothlórien a living
hub.

### Campaign-gated secrets — the return loop

The Mirror always holds more than can currently be seen. Campaign progress is
the key that reveals more of it. Some visions are unreadable until later events
make them legible — you return as new questions make new pasts visible.

The past is a **knowledge resource you mine by returning.** The campaign poses
a mystery the present cannot answer, and the answer lives only in a vision the
player travels to witness.

### Frequency

Rare set-pieces — a handful across the whole game, each a famous haunting
moment. Not a grindable content type.

**Ration the load-bearing ones.** Most visions are optional depth (rare recipes,
skills, lore). A few pivotal ones are genuine required campaign keys, used
sparingly so the Mirror stays special rather than a mandatory turnstile.

## "Things That Yet May Be" — Open Question

Tolkien's Mirror also shows possible futures: things that *may be* but might
never come to pass. Galadriel warns they might not. This opens a richer design
space:

- A vision of a possible future the player's later real actions might
  avert or fulfill.
- More complex than past-only visions; requires the campaign to have
  meaningful player-driven choices.

Not decided. Resolve when the campaign narrative design opens.

## Tone Notes

Mirrors land best as *events and forces* witnessed — the fall in Moria, the
Watcher, a battle's distant fury — because a vision of a scene is more
haunting than a staged conversation. Characters speaking in a vision is fully
allowed if a given Mirror wants it; do whatever serves the moment. But lean
toward showing forces rather than staging dialogues.

All flavor text inside a Mirror follows `design/voice-tone.md`. These are the
most tonally demanding moments in the game; the register discipline matters
most here.
