# HearthCraft

An offline idle RPG for Android set in high fantasy — built for the love of Lord of the Rings Online, the Ettenmoors, strategy, and the crafting identity that makes support roles feel indispensable.

---

## How It Started

HearthCraft began as a cooking idle game — a small, personal project inspired by Tristin's [IdleFantasy](https://github.com/tristin/IdleFanasy), which showed what a well-designed idle loop could feel like on Android.

The seed idea was simple: what if you played the person who keeps the fighters alive? Not the warrior. Not the mage. The one in the back who knows that the right meal before a hard march changes everything. A specialist identity — the warlock-culinarian, potion master and chef in one — who is the hidden engine of a roving band of fighters.

That cooking game is still here. It is still being built. But the scope has grown.

---

## What It Became

Somewhere along the way, the Ettenmoors got involved.

LotRO's contested PvP region — the troll-fells north of Rivendell, the borderland between Angmar and the free peoples — is one of the most atmospheric places in any game ever made. That feeling of contested, dangerous, meaningful ground: draws that feel like victories, retreats that feel like defeats, the slow ebb and flow of a front that never quite closes. That's what HearthCraft is reaching toward.

Add to that a love of strategy games, party RPGs, and the deep Tolkien lore that LotRO drew from — the Angmar Wars, the Barrow-downs, Carn Dûm, the tragedy of Fornost — and the game grew into something much larger than a cooking sim.

HearthCraft is now designed as two interconnected games sharing one economy. The cooking game is the foundation. The raid RPG is the destination.

---

## The Two Games

### Game 1 — The Provisioning Loop *(being built now)*

You are a warlock-culinarian. You gather wild ingredients and tend a farm and garden. You cook recipes from a growing repertoire you discovered yourself, and brew alchemical preparations with properties that blur the line between food and sorcery. You provision a band of fighters before sending them into the world.

The kitchen is the engine. Everything runs on what you make.

The line between a perfect dish and a potion is a matter of intent. Your food is borderline magical — not metaphorically. Ingredients have power; preparation unlocks it; feeding someone the right thing at the right moment changes what they're capable of. The band doesn't know if it's cuisine or sorcery. They don't care. It works.

**This is not a cozy cooking sim.** It is a specialist identity fantasy rooted in craft and deep knowledge.

### Game 2 — The Raid RPG *(future)*

A party combat RPG fought across named battlegrounds drawn from the history of Middle-earth. The Barrow-downs. The Fall of Fornost. Holding the Ettenmoors. The Assault on Carn Dûm. Helm's Deep. The Pelennor. The Black Gate. The mythic evacuation of Gondolin.

The player never fights. The band fights. Your role is what it has always been: provision the party for what they're about to face.

**Your cooking is the raid's sustain mechanic.** The band eats continuously through hours-long engagements. Provisions are a supply train, not a pre-battle buff. Run out mid-raid and things go wrong — not as a penalty, but as a consequence that the whole preceding game was training you to avoid.

The two halves are one game because of this connection. The mastered poultice that saves a life in the raid RPG is the same mastery system being built in the kitchen today.

## Current State

HearthCraft V1 is in active development. The provisioning loop is being built first — it is not a prototype to be replaced, it is the permanent foundation everything else will extend.

**What exists today:**
- A working provisioning loop: farm plot, four independent garden slots, forage sessions with seed drops
- A kitchen with recipe discovery through ingredient experimentation
- Probability-based missions with wound consequences and permanent member loss
- Color-coded difficulty, flavor text, and band member personalities
- An intro screen with opening lore, band selection, and welcome quotes
- Four starting bands: Druid Circle, Dwarven Company, Corsair Fleet, and the Greycloaks

**What's coming in V1:** full recipe discovery engine, expanded mission tiers, alchemy groundwork.

## The Four Bands

The player aligns with one band at the start. The choice is permanent and sets the tone of the entire world — mission names, ingredient character, the culture you operate within. All bands are equivalent in power. The differences are identity.

- **The Mithlost** — Ancient forest elves. Patient, sorrowful, deadlier than their gentleness suggests. They stayed to fight a long defeat when others sailed West.
- **The Undermarch** — Mountain dwarves. Grim, loyal, stone-deep. They will not complain about a hard march, but they notice — and remember — who kept them fed.
- **The Freewake** — Sea corsairs. Chaotic, adaptable, opportunistic. Answer to no flag. Learned the right meal before a hard job changes everything.
- **The Greycloaks** — Borderland wardens. Watchful, unaffiliated, worn. At the edges of civilization long enough that the edges feel like home.

Each band has eight fully named members — 32 characters across the full roster — with distinct personalities, food preferences, and roles. They are not faceless stats. They are people. Keeping them well-provisioned is personal.

## The Raid RPG — Vision in Full

### The Battlegrounds

Fourteen named scenarios drawn from the history of Middle-earth, in two eras:

**The Angmar Wars (Third Age ~1409–1975)**
1. Cleansing of the Barrow-downs — haunted ground to restore, not an army to fight
2. The Fall of Fornost — a doomed last stand, scored on how long the Dúnedain hold
3. Holding the Ettenmoors — the contested borderland, the "put yourself to the test" ground
4. The Host of the West at Fornost — the reconquest; a pincer you must provision separately
5. Storming of Angmar / Assault on Carn Dûm — the frozen citadel at the roof of the world

**The War of the Ring (T.A. 3018–3019) and beyond**
6. The Paths of the Dead — not a fight; a test of nerve through terror itself
7. Battle under the Trees / Mirkwood — a defense of living forest against fire and Orcs
8. The Cleansing of Dol Guldur — heal the land a fortress poisoned; the endgame cleansing
9. Helm's Deep — hold the Hornburg through the desperate night until dawn
10. The Battle of the Pelennor Fields — the sprawling hinge of the age
11. The Black Gate / Morannon — a hopeless diversion fought so someone else can finish it
12. Battle of Dale / Siege of Erebor — the battle nobody sings about that quietly saved everything
13. Battle of Azanulbizar — the bloodiest day in Dwarven history; victory measured in who you bring home
14. The Fall of Gondolin — *[First Age, mythic register]* provision the evacuation; success is survivors

### How a Raid Works

The player's agency is in preparation and composition. The raid then resolves over real-world time, narrating itself through a live event log. This is an idle game: provision, send, come back later.

The combat model is a race between two pools — enemy HP vs. your party's effective survivability, stretched by healing and by how long provisions last. Four roles, each essential:

- **Tank** — holds space and attention; heroic peak: the Last Stand
- **Healer** — sustains life and counters corruption; heroic peak: the Pull-Back (snatch a fallen member back from death mid-raid)
- **Damage** — drives kill speed, exploits enemy vulnerabilities; heroic peak: the Slaying (fell a Bane in a single legendary blow)
- **Captain** — the fourth role; a force-multiplier and dread-resistance that makes the trinity work near the Nine; heroic peak: Inspiration

**Inspiration** is the Tolkien theme made mechanical. When the resolution projects a loss, a member can become borderline supernatural — Beren in Angband, Éowyn against the Witch-king, Sam carrying Frodo. Not a reliable cooldown. Grace. The Captain makes it far more likely; any member can theoretically catch fire; the whole company rising at once is rarer still and more powerful.

Inspiration can't beat the apex. Against the Nine it converts a Defeat into a Stalemate — survival, not victory. The matter remains unsettled. The Ettenmoors feeling: draws that feel like victories, coming back stronger until stalemates become wins.

### The Enemy

Five canonical tiers, power and untouchability kept separate:
- **Rabble** — orcs, Easterlings, hired swords; driven forward, dangerous in numbers
- **The Fell** — great warg-riders, orc captains, barrow-wights; dangerous individually
- **Banes** — dragons, wight-lords, ancient things; felling one is a legendary deed
- **The Nine** — the Nazgûl; never killed; only survived or repelled; their Dread aura is what makes the host dangerous, and the Captain's sole purpose near them is to counter it
- **Nameless Things** — beneath Moria; not a combat target at all

Enemy leadership projects a Dread aura that buffs its host and breaks your party's will. The tactical answer to a Nazgûl-led host is not to fight the Nazgûl — it is to repel it, collapsing the aura and watching the orcs it held together falter. The canonical beat: the host of Mordor breaking when the Witch-king falls.

### Hidden Requirements — The Clue System

Requirements are never stated. The Barrow-downs reads cold, draining, deathly — infer what the band needs. A failed run returns after-action text naming the nature of failure, not numbers: *"The cold sank into them. Aldric could not get warm."* Band members comment before runs and after. The player converges by attempting. Lore engagement is rewarded, not required.

### The Influence Map

A map of Middle-earth where shadow recedes and light returns as battlegrounds are cleared. The reward that isn't a number. The world visibly healing because of what you built in the kitchen. When a battleground is lost, the cost can be lost ground — shadow re-spreading — rather than lost characters. The punishment that stings without emptying the roster.

### Wounds, the Houses of Healing, and Alchemy's Purpose

Ordinary wounds heal with time and the right food — back in the player's domain. Grievous wounds require the Houses of Healing, a healer of sufficient power, a rare alchemical preparation discovered and mastered by the player, and a lot of time. The wounded member is out of the war for a meaningful stretch. You fight short-handed.

*Raw athelas is a worthless weed until the right hands prepare it.* The cure is a discovered and mastered preparation — the same discovery and mastery system built in the kitchen, now with life-or-death weight. The healer cannot heal without the player's preparation. The preparation cannot heal without the healer. Two indispensable roles; neither sufficient alone. The player's identity holds even at the deepest moment of crisis.

## What the Future Looks Like

- **V2** — Alchemy as a fully built parallel crafting track; expanded eight-member rosters; multi-member provisioning
- **V3** — Deeper recipe discovery; ingredient rarities; the store and seed economy maturing
- **V4** — Band member growth through provisioning and the deeds they survive; wounds and the Houses of Healing
- **V5+** — The full raid RPG: battlegrounds, bestiary, the four-role combat system, the live event log, the influence map of Middle-earth healing as shadow recedes

The shadow in the south-east is not idle. The world is in slow decay. What the player builds in the kitchen is not just comfort — it is the thing standing between the band and what is coming.

---

## What This Game Is Not — Ever

- No combat for the player — the band fights so you never have to
- No multiplayer, trading, or leaderboards
- No cloud saves or accounts
- No ads, IAP, or real-money transactions of any kind
- No internet requirement — ever
- No energy systems or artificial time pressure

## Build

Requires Android Studio (JBR included) or a standalone JDK 11+.

```bash
./gradlew build
```

Minimum SDK: API 26 (Android 8.0). No Google Play Services required. F-Droid compatible.

- **Language**: Kotlin
- **UI**: Jetpack Compose + Material 3
- **Database**: Room
- **Background work**: WorkManager
- **DI**: Hilt
- **Serialization**: kotlinx.serialization
- **Architecture**: MVVM + Repository
